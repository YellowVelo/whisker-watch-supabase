PRODUCT PRINCIPLES (Final Version)
Purpose: Define how product decisions are made
Audience: Product, UX, Engineering, AI, Contributors
Product Principles
Product Principles define how decisions are made within Wysker Watch. They are durable decision filters that guide every feature, workflow, interaction, and implementation.
If a proposal conflicts with these principles, the proposal—not the principles—should be reconsidered.
How These Principles Are Used
Every future proposal should be evaluated by asking:
•	Does this reduce owner effort?
•	Does this improve understanding?
•	Does this strengthen veterinary conversations?
•	Does this preserve trust?
•	Does this help tell the pet’s health story?
If not, the proposal should be redesigned, simplified, postponed, or rejected.
Core Decision Principles
1. Behavior Over Diagnosis
Owners record what they can confidently observe. The product should never require medical interpretation.
2. Individual Baselines First
Every pet has its own normal. Meaningful change is measured relative to the individual pet.
3. Longitudinal Stories Over Isolated Events
Patterns over time create meaningful understanding. Features should prioritize continuity, trends, and context.
4. Insight Over Data Collection
Information should only be collected if it contributes to understanding.
5. Log Changes, Not Routines
Routine behavior becomes baseline. Owners should primarily record what has changed.
6. Missing Data Is Meaningful
The system must distinguish between:
•	Nothing happened
•	Nothing was logged
•	Logging was intentionally skipped
Veterinary Principles
7. Veterinarians Are Partners
Features should strengthen—not replace—the owner–veterinarian relationship.
8. Observations Before Conclusions
Reports should summarize observations, trends, and timelines—not medical conclusions.
9. Preserve Context
Medical events should always be presented alongside relevant behavioral and historical context.
10. Honest Uncertainty
When the system is uncertain, it must say so clearly.
AI Principles
11. AI Supports Understanding
AI should summarize history, identify trends, explain changes, prepare reports, and answer questions about recorded information.
12. AI Does Not Diagnose
AI must never diagnose disease, recommend medications, replace veterinary advice, or create unnecessary urgency.
13. AI Should Explain Its Reasoning
Transparency builds trust.
14. AI Must Respect Emotional Context
Responses should remain calm, empathetic, and responsible.
Data Principles
15. Store Observations as Primary Truth
Everything else should be derived when possible.
16. Preserve Historical Integrity
Past observations should never be rewritten.
17. One Observation, Many Uses
A single observation may support multiple insights.
18. Every Observation Belongs to a Story
Data should never exist in isolation.
19. Favor Reusable Models
Reusable behavior libraries should be preferred over condition specific implementations.
Engineering Principles
20. Build for Reuse
Favor reusable systems over specialized one offs.
21. Extend Before Creating
Extend existing patterns unless a new one is clearly justified.
22. Preserve Simplicity
Technical complexity should remain behind the interface.
23. Protect Historical Integrity
Version changes must not invalidate previously collected information.
24. Principles Override Convenience
The easiest implementation is not always the correct one.
Roadmap Principles
25. Prioritize Work That:
•	Reduces owner effort
•	Improves consistency
•	Strengthens veterinary conversations
•	Improves change detection
•	Reinforces baseline understanding
•	Simplifies workflows
•	Increases trust
Decision Tradeoffs
If principles conflict:
•	Simplicity > completeness
•	Transparency > automation
•	Owner effort reduction > richer data
•	Honest uncertainty > overstated confidence
•	Reduced logging > abandonment risk
•	Clarity > cleverness
•	Usability > more features
Decision Hierarchy
Prioritize in this order:
1.	Protect the pet’s health
2.	Preserve owner trust
3.	Reduce owner effort
4.	Strengthen veterinary partnerships
5.	Improve meaningful insight
6.	Improve long term engagement
7.	Add new functionality
What Wysker Watch Will Never Become
•	A diagnostic engine
•	A replacement for veterinarians
•	A source of unnecessary fear
•	A data collection platform without purpose
•	A product that rewards obsessive tracking
•	A feature heavy product that sacrifices usability
•	A product driven by competitors instead of principles
Definition of Success
These principles succeed when they make future decisions easier, remove ambiguity, and protect the product from unnecessary complexity or drift.
