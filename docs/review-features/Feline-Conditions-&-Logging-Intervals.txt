This is actually the direction I would take for Wysker Watch. Pet owners generally **can't diagnose symptoms**, but they **can observe behaviors**. The app should ask things like *"Did your cat leave food in the bowl?"* instead of *"Is your cat nauseated?"*

## A design recommendation for Wysker Watch

Rather than asking owners to complete long condition-specific checklists, I'd build a **single library of observable behaviors**, with each behavior linked to one or more conditions behind the scenes.

For example:

| Observable behavior  | Supports monitoring for                                   |
| -------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| Eats less than usual | CKD, IBD, dental disease, cancer, liver disease           |
| Vomits               | CKD, IBD, hyperthyroidism, pancreatitis, food intolerance |
| Drinking more water  | CKD, diabetes, hyperthyroidism                            |
| Losing weight        | CKD, hyperthyroidism, diabetes, cancer, IBD               |
| Hesitates to jump    | Arthritis, injury, neurologic disease                     |
| Hiding more          | Pain, CKD, fever, cancer, stress                          |

This approach has several advantages:

* The owner only logs a behavior once, even if it applies to multiple diagnoses.
* The app can automatically generate condition-specific trends and insights.
* As new diagnoses are added, existing behavior history becomes immediately useful without asking owners to change how they log data.

I think this behavior-first model is one of the strongest differentiators for Wysker Watch because it mirrors how veterinary clinicians gather history: they start with **what the owner observed**, then interpret those observations in the context of the pet's known conditions.
